To what extent can we trust scientific findings?

Posted on 2019-12-16 | In Reproducibility

Abstract

Concerns about a lack of reproducibility of statistically significant results have recently been raised in many fields. Then the question of how to quantify the reproducibility follows.

Introduction

Scientific discovery centers on empirical testing of research hypotheses. P-value is the standard tool to interpret results in statistical hypothesis testing. How does the standard tool work? A result associated with a P-value below a predefined significance level (typically 0.05) is considered as “statistically significant” and interpreted as evidence in favour of a hypothesis.

The problem with inference based on P-value is that it provides only partial information about the probability of a tested hypothesis being true. As a matter of fact,this probability depends on the statistical power to detect a true positive effect and the prior probability that the hypothesis is true. (e.g. low statistical power increases the probability that a statistically significant effect is false positive, which means that statistically significant results from small studies are more likely to be false positive. Similarly, a lower prior probability for a hypothesis to be true would increase the probability that a statistically significant effect is a false positive). What’s more, this problem is exacerbated by publication bias in favor of speculative findings and against null results.

However, Reproducibility of statistically significant results have recently raised in fields like: medicine, neuroscience, genetics, psychology and economics. The mismatch between the interpretation of statistically significant findings and a lack of reproducibility threatens to undermine the validity of statistical hypothesis testing as it is currently practised in many research fields.

Typically, the judgement of credibility of published results is left to individual researchers.

Example

The prediction markets predict the outcomes of the replications well and work as a promising tool for assessing the reproducibility of published scientific results in the field of psychology.
